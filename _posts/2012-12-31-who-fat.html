--- layout: post blog-title: Who Fat? author: Joe Kutner author-link: https://twitter.com/codefinger --- This week, Drew Brees became the first NFL quarterback to throw for more than 5000 yards in three consecutive seasons -- but I'm not cheering for him.

Drew Brees is the co-chair of the President's Council on Fitness, Sports and Nutrition (PCFSN) -- an organization that encourages healthy lifestyles for children. Its mission is to promote good nutrition, but that isn't stopping Mr. Brees from being the face of Pepsi.

Even worse, Brees appears in advertisments alongside the boy-band One Direction. These commercials are directly targeting the same children that the PCFSN is trying to educate. The sponsership is a clear conflict of interest for Brees.

Soft drinks, and specifically the high levels of fructose sugar they contain, have been identified by scientists and researchers as a predominate contributor to childhood obesity.

In one study, 641 children who drank 8-ounces of sugar sweetened beverages each day for 18 months gained more than 2 pounds of additional weight and accumulated more fat than their peers in the control group who drank artificially sweetened drinks. Another study found that the risk of becoming obese increased 1.6 times for each sugar-sweeted drink a child consumed. Even more damning, a randomized controlled trial published in the British Journal of Medicine found that a modest reduction in carbonated drinks led to a nearly 8% differental in obesity levels after 12 months.

The science linking soft drinks to obesity goes on and on. They've also been linked to type 2 diabetes and even cancer. But our consumption of these beverages continues to rise -- and obesity with it.

Acoording to the USADA, per-capita soft drink consumption has increased 500% in the last fifty years. In that same time, obesity has increased 100%. More than 12.5 million kids and 78 million adults in America are now obese.

Sonia Capiro, an endocrinologist at Yale University, claims that reducing the comsumption of sugar-sweetened drinks like soda could reverse the astonishing increasing in childhood obesity. She wrote in an editorial for the New England Journal of Medicine that "the time has come to take action."

Certainly, the time has come for Drew Brees to make a decision: step down from the PCFSN, or publicly reonounce his Pepsi sponsership.

I hope that when my son is an adult he looks back at Drew Brees' Pepsi commercials the same way I look back at cigarette advertisements featuring Babe Ruth, Joe Dimaggio and Ted Williams.
